1. Overview and Physiological Principles

The PiCCO (Pulse index Continuous Cardiac Output) system is an advanced haemodynamic monitoring platform that combines two complementary technologies: **transpulmonary thermodilution (TPTD)** for intermittent calibration and **pulse contour analysis (PCA)** for continuous beat-to-beat monitoring. Together, these provide a far more comprehensive haemodynamic picture than standard arterial lines or central venous pressure alone.

1.1 Transpulmonary Thermodilution (TPTD)

A cold indicator bolus (typically 15 mL of 0–8°C normal saline) is injected via a central venous catheter. The cold passes through the right heart, pulmonary vasculature, left heart, and is detected by a thermistor-tipped arterial catheter, usually positioned in the femoral artery. The resulting temperature-time curve is analysed using the **Stewart-Hamilton equation** to derive cardiac output (CO).

Beyond CO, the shape and transit characteristics of the thermodilution curve allow derivation of volumetric parameters reflecting the thermal mixing volumes encountered — giving rise to unique parameters such as Global End-Diastolic Volume (GEDV) and Extravascular Lung Water (EVLW) that cannot be measured by pulmonary artery catheters.

1.2 Pulse Contour Analysis (PCA)

Once calibrated via TPTD, the PiCCO system continuously calculates cardiac output by analysing the morphology of each arterial pressure waveform. Stroke volume is estimated from the area under the systolic portion of the arterial pressure curve, accounting for aortic impedance and compliance (derived during calibration). This yields **continuous, real-time CO** — a capability not available with pulmonary artery catheters.

PCA also derives dynamic parameters including Stroke Volume Variation (SVV) and Pulse Pressure Variation (PPV) from respiratory-induced beat-to-beat changes. These are powerful predictors of fluid responsiveness under appropriate conditions.

2. Indications, Contraindications, and Patient Selection

2.1 Indications

- **Complex or refractory haemodynamic instability:** especially where the aetiology is unclear (e.g., mixed septic and cardiogenic shock, post-cardiac surgery)
- **Fluid management in ARDS:** EVLWI provides direct quantification of pulmonary oedema beyond what chest X-ray or clinical examination can offer
- **Goal-directed therapy protocols:** where volumetric preload parameters (GEDVI, ITBVI) are preferred over filling pressures (CVP, PAOP)
- **Monitoring response to vasoactive agents:** continuous CO and SVR tracking during titration of vasopressors or inotropes
- **High-risk surgical patients:** perioperative haemodynamic optimisation
- **PAC unavailable or contraindicated:** PiCCO offers less invasive advanced monitoring as an alternative

2.2 Absolute and Relative Contraindications

⚠️ **CONTRAINDICATIONS — Use with Extreme Caution or Avoid**

- Intracardiac or intrapulmonary shunts (L-to-R or R-to-L): invalidates thermodilution mixing assumptions
- Intra-aortic balloon pump (IABP): pulse contour analysis is unreliable; TPTD may still be used
- Significant aortic regurgitation: prolonged washout curve distorts CO calculation
- Large aortic aneurysm: expands thermal mixing volume, artificially elevating GEDV and ITBV
- Pneumonectomy: EVLW is underestimated — only perfused lung is measured
- Tricuspid or pulmonary valve regurgitation: prolongs thermodilution curve, overestimates CO
- Active therapeutic or accidental hypothermia: reduced ΔT° reduces accuracy (minimum ΔT° 0.2°C required)
- Arterial grafting or infection at intended access site: contraindication to arterial catheter insertion

⚠️ **CONDITIONS REQUIRING CAUTIOUS INTERPRETATION**

- Atrial fibrillation or frequent ectopy: reduces reliability of SVV and PPV
- Spontaneous breathing or low tidal volumes (<8 mL/kg): invalidates SVV/PPV for fluid responsiveness
- PEEP changes: alter EVLWI measurement and may falsely increase or decrease readings
- Obesity: EVLWI underestimated when indexed to actual body weight — always use predicted body weight
- Significant pleural or pericardial effusions: act as thermal sinks, underestimating EVLW

3. Equipment and Insertion Procedure

3.1 Required Equipment

- **PiCCO arterial catheter:** thermistor-tipped, typically 4–5 Fr, 20 cm (femoral) or 22 cm (brachial); paediatric sizes also available
- **Standard CVC** — ideally jugular or subclavian (see Section 5 for femoral CVC considerations)
- **PiCCO Monitoring Kit:** inline injectate temperature sensor, pressure transducer set
- **PiCCO monitor** (PulsioFlex/Pulsiocare platform) or compatible integrated module (Philips IntelliVue, GE CARESCAPE, Dräger, Mindray)
- **Cold saline boluses** (0–8°C, typically 15 mL for adults)

3.2 Arterial Catheter Insertion — Step-by-Step

Site Selection

|Site|Catheter Length|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|Femoral artery (standard)|20 cm|Most common; best signal-to-noise for thermodilution|
|Axillary/brachial artery|22 cm|Acceptable alternative when femoral access contraindicated|
|Radial artery|50 cm specialist catheter|Must remain fully advanced — withdrawal >5 cm renders TPTD inaccurate|

Procedure (Femoral — Standard)

1. Prepare the femoral area with **full sterile barrier precautions**
2. Identify femoral artery via anatomical landmarks or **ultrasound guidance** (strongly recommended to reduce failed first attempts and haematoma risk)
3. Use the **Seldinger technique:** 18G needle → guidewire → dilator → 20 cm thermistor catheter
4. Advance fully to the hub — catheter tip should lie in the **iliac artery or abdominal aorta** for optimal signal detection
5. Secure catheter and connect to PiCCO pressure transducer and monitoring cable
6. **Zero the transducer** at the phlebostatic axis; confirm adequate arterial waveform
7. **Enter patient data** into monitor: height, weight, sex, age (required for predicted body weight calculations)

CVC Setup for Thermodilution

- Place inline injectate temperature sensor as close as possible to the **distal lumen of the CVC**, outside infusion lines via a stopcock
- This sensor measures the exact temperature of the injectate — critical for accurate ΔT° calculation
- Preferred CVC sites: **internal jugular or subclavian** — see Section 6.2 regarding femoral CVC pitfalls

4. Calibration Protocol and Operational Use

4.1 Initial Calibration (Transpulmonary Thermodilution)

Calibration must be performed before pulse contour analysis parameters can be trusted. The procedure should be performed **three times within 10 minutes**, and the average used.

1. Ensure patient is haemodynamically stable enough for the procedure
2. Confirm arterial waveform is optimal: square wave test, no damping, transducer zeroed and levelled
3. Prepare **15 mL cold normal saline boluses** (cooled to 0–8°C)
4. Inject each bolus **rapidly and smoothly over < 4 seconds** through the proximal CVC lumen
5. Monitor the thermodilution curve on screen — reject abnormal curves (see Section 6.1)
6. Accept three consistent measurements (coefficient of variation **<10%** between readings)

4.2 Recalibration Frequency

**Minimum:** 2–3 times per 24 hours in stable patients.

**Mandatory recalibration after:**

- Position changes
- Significant fluid boluses
- Initiation or weaning of vasoactive agents
- PEEP changes
- Any episode of haemodynamic instability
- Temperature change or progression of sepsis

⚠️ **CRITICAL CALIBRATION RULE** PCCO becomes increasingly unreliable between recalibrations if vascular resistance changes. A rising discrepancy between PCCO and the last TPTD CO should always prompt recalibration, not documentation alone. If a single thermodilution measurement differs by >15% from the other two in a triplicate, discard it and repeat.

4.3 What Each Technology Measures

|Thermodilution Parameters (Intermittent)|Pulse Contour Parameters (Continuous)|
|---|---|
|Cardiac Output / Index (CO/CI) — calibrated|Pulse Contour CO (PCCO/PCCI) — continuous|
|Global End-Diastolic Volume Index (GEDVI)|Systemic Vascular Resistance Index (SVRI)|
|Intrathoracic Blood Volume Index (ITBVI)|Stroke Volume / Stroke Volume Index (SV/SVI)|
|Extravascular Lung Water Index (EVLWI)|Stroke Volume Variation (SVV)|
|Pulmonary Vascular Permeability Index (PVPI)|Pulse Pressure Variation (PPV)|
|Global Ejection Fraction (GEF)|dPmax (LV contractility index)|

6. Known Pitfalls and Interpretation Errors

This section represents the most critical content for clinical safety. The following are the most commonly encountered errors in PiCCO interpretation, grouped by category.

6.1 Thermodilution Technical Errors

⚠️ **ABNORMAL THERMODILUTION CURVE MORPHOLOGY**

- **Kilimanjaro curve** (tall, narrow peak): caused by too-rapid injection or inadequate mixing. **Overestimates CO.** Reject and repeat.
- **Table Mountain curve** (flat plateau): caused by slow injection or catheter kinking. **Underestimates CO.** Reject and repeat.
- **Minimum ΔT° of 0.2°C required:** in hypothermic or febrile patients, the signal may be too small. Use well-chilled saline and ensure rapid injection.
- **Insufficient interval between injections:** if injections are repeated too quickly, baseline blood temperature rises — wait at least 60–90 seconds between boluses.
- **Inline sensor misplacement:** the injectate temperature sensor must be placed outside infusion lines and as close as possible to the distal CVC lumen. Misplacement introduces systematic error in all derived thermodilution parameters.

6.2 Femoral CVC Access — A Major Systematic Error

⚠️ **CRITICAL: Femoral CVC Injection Overestimates GEDVI**

When the cold indicator is injected via a **femoral CVC** rather than a jugular/subclavian CVC, the additional thermal mixing volume of the inferior vena cava is included in the thermodilution computation.

This results in significant **OVERESTIMATION of GEDVI** (and therefore ITBVI) — creating a false appearance of adequate or excessive preload when the patient may actually be volume-depleted.

The PiCCO-2 algorithm includes a correction for femoral CVC injection, but this correction **must be enabled** by correctly entering the CVC site in monitor settings. Failure to do so produces systematically incorrect GEDVI values.

PVPI and GEF are also affected if GEDV is incorrect. Verify CVC site settings whenever femoral venous access is used.

**Recommendation: use jugular or subclavian CVC for TPTD injection whenever possible.**

6.3 SVV and PPV — Conditions Invalidating Dynamic Parameters

SVV and PPV are only valid under a narrow set of conditions. Using them outside these conditions is a major source of interpretive error.

⚠️ **SVV/PPV ARE UNRELIABLE IN:**

- Spontaneous breathing or assisted modes (PSV, SIMV): respiratory variation is effort-dependent, not volume-responsive
- Tidal volumes **< 8 mL/kg** predicted body weight: insufficient pressure swings to reliably induce preload variation
- Atrial fibrillation or frequent arrhythmias: beat-to-beat variation reflects rhythm, not preload
- Intra-abdominal hypertension (IAP >12 mmHg): reduces compliance, falsely elevating variation
- Right ventricular failure or severe pulmonary hypertension: RV afterload mismatch dominates
- Open chest conditions: loss of pleural pressure variation
- High PEEP (>10 cmH₂O): may falsely increase variation independent of volume status
- Low HR:RR ratio (<3.6): insufficient cardiac cycles per breath

6.4 EVLWI — Pitfalls in Interpretation

- **Obesity:** EVLWI must be indexed to **predicted body weight (PBW)**, not actual weight. Using actual weight in obese patients underestimates EVLWI, potentially masking severe pulmonary oedema.
- **Pneumonectomy / unilateral lung disease:** only perfused lung is included in EVLW measurement. Post-pneumonectomy states significantly underestimate total lung water.
- **Pleural effusions:** large pleural effusions act as thermal sinks, causing underestimation of EVLW.
- **Elevated PEEP:** redistributes pulmonary blood flow and can either raise or lower measured EVLWI depending on the net haemodynamic effect.
- **EVLWI vs CXR:** clinical studies consistently show CXR underestimates lung water. An EVLWI >10 mL/kg in the context of a normal-appearing CXR should **not** be dismissed — ultrasound confirmation is recommended.

6.5 Pulse Contour Analysis — Sources of Drift and Error

- PCA requires recalibration whenever vascular tone changes significantly — a rising PCCO following vasopressor dose change does not reliably reflect true CO without a new TPTD.
- **IABP:** PCA is invalid during balloon counterpulsation. TPTD may still be performed but timing must account for balloon cycling.
- **Aortic regurgitation:** regurgitant volume is included in the area under the systolic curve, overestimating forward SV and CO.
- **Peripheral vasoconstriction:** damped waveform reduces PCA accuracy. Ensure optimal waveform morphology before calibration.
- **Damped waveform:** even minor kinking, clot, or air in the arterial line tubing affects PCA — perform square wave test prior to thermodilution.

6.6 Derived Parameters — Compounding Error

⚠️ **CAUTION WITH DERIVED PARAMETERS (GEF, PVPI, CFI)**

GEF, PVPI, and CFI are mathematical ratios of other measured values. Errors in primary measurements are compounded in the derived parameter.

- **GEF = 4 × SV / GEDV:** if GEDV is overestimated (e.g., femoral CVC), GEF will be falsely reduced, potentially mimicking ventricular dysfunction.
- **PVPI = EVLW / (0.25 × GEDV):** similarly affected by GEDV overestimation — a correct PVPI requires an accurate GEDV.

Always verify the quality of underlying measurements before acting on abnormal derived values. Clinician assessment of EVLWI and GEDVI **independently** is often preferable to relying on ratio-derived parameters alone.

6.7 Summary: Conditions Rendering PiCCO Inaccurate

|Condition|Effect on Parameters|
|---|---|
|Intracardiac shunt|CO overestimated (L→R shunt)|
|Aortic aneurysm|GEDV/ITBV overestimated|
|Pneumonectomy|EVLW underestimated|
|IABP in situ|PCA unreliable|
|Severe aortic regurgitation|CO/SV overestimated|
|Hypothermia (<35°C)|ΔT° may be insufficient (<0.2°C)|
|Femoral CVC (uncorrected)|GEDVI overestimated|
|Spontaneous breathing|SVV/PPV invalid|

7. Clinical Decision Framework

7.1 Haemodynamic Targets

|Parameter|Target Range|
|---|---|
|CI / PCCI|> 3.0 L/min/m²|
|GEDVI (preload)|680–800 mL/m²|
|ITBVI|850–1000 mL/m²|
|EVLWI|3–7 mL/kg (PBW)|
|SVV / PPV (if valid)|< 10% / < 13%|
|SVRI|1700–2400 dyn·s·cm⁻⁵·m²|
|ScvO₂|70–80%|

7.2 Diagnostic Approach to Haemodynamic Instability

1. **Confirm waveform quality** — square wave test, zero transducer, check for damping
2. **Perform TPTD calibration (×3)** — review each curve morphology before accepting
3. **Assess volume status:** GEDVI and ITBVI (static) ± SVV/PPV if mechanically ventilated in sinus rhythm with TV ≥8 mL/kg
4. **Assess cardiac output and contractility:** CI, PCCI, dPmax, GEF
5. **Assess afterload:** SVRI — guide vasopressor vs vasodilator decisions
6. **Assess pulmonary oedema:** EVLWI — consider fluid restriction or diuresis if >10 mL/kg
7. **Correlate with ScvO₂, lactate, and clinical examination** — do not act on PiCCO data in isolation

7.3 ARDS-Specific Considerations

- EVLWI > 10 mL/kg = clinically significant pulmonary oedema; guides fluid restriction even without overt haemodynamic compromise
- PVPI ≥ 3 suggests **permeability oedema** (consistent with ARDS/DAD) rather than hydrostatic oedema — important for aetiology-directed management
- EVLWI correlates with ARDS severity, ventilator days, ICU LOS, and mortality — use as a monitoring parameter to track treatment response
- Do not interpret EVLWI in isolation; combine with bedside ultrasound and clinical findings

8. Device Platform Considerations

8.1 Monitor Platforms

- **Standalone:** PulsioFlex / Pulsiocare (Getinge) — latest generation, integrates PiCCO and ProAQT in one device
- **Integrated modules:** Philips IntelliVue, GE CARESCAPE, Dräger, Mindray — same PiCCO algorithm with familiar bedside interfaces

8.2 GE E-PiCCO Module — Important Interoperability Warning

⚠️ **GE E-PiCCO vs Getinge PiCCO: Values Are NOT Interchangeable**

A 2023 multicentre study (_Scientific Reports_) showed that GE E-PiCCO and Getinge PiCCO produce comparable Cardiac Index values (both TPTD and PCA), but diverge significantly for **EVLWI, SVRI, SVV, and PPV**.

GEDVI agreement was adequate only for jugular CVC injection on the GE device. Femoral CVC without correction is not supported by GE E-PiCCO, unlike the Getinge PiCCO-2 algorithm.

Clinical decision-making based on threshold values validated with one device should **not** be directly applied if the other is in use. When transferring a patient between units using different monitor platforms, always recalibrate on the receiving device and do not assume numeric equivalence of non-CO parameters.

8.3 MRI Safety

In vitro 3T MRI testing showed no significant heating (<0.3°C) or catheter displacement of PiCCO catheters. However, this was bench testing — the clinical decision to leave or remove the catheter prior to MRI should account for individual patient risk of catheter removal vs MRI necessity.

9. Practical Tips for Daily Use

- Always enter **predicted body weight** (from height and sex), not actual weight — affects EVLWI, GEDVI, and ITBVI indexation
- Use a **dedicated CVC lumen** for thermodilution injection — do not inject through a lumen running continuous infusions without flushing first
- Keep cold saline boluses in an ice bath at the bedside; both volume consistency and temperature consistency matter
- In haemodynamically unstable patients, perform TPTD **every 1–2 hours** or after each significant intervention, not just twice daily
- If only one parameter appears abnormal, **reassess technical factors** before initiating therapy
- Use the PiCCO haemodynamic decision tree (provided with the device) as a structured framework — particularly useful for less experienced operators
- Document the **CVC site correctly in monitor settings** — this is the single most correctable source of GEDVI error
- Combine PiCCO data with **bedside echocardiography** when available — complementary tools reduce diagnostic uncertainty

10. Discontinuation

- Remove PiCCO catheter when: patient is haemodynamically stable for ≥48 hours without vasopressors, catheter-related infection is suspected, or monitoring is no longer clinically indicated
- **Maximum recommended dwell time: 10 days** (vs 72 hours for pulmonary artery catheters)
- Apply firm manual pressure for at least **10 minutes** following femoral catheter removal; longer if coagulopathic
- Document post-removal neurovascular observations for femoral access (distal pulses, skin colour and temperature)
